Chain Reactions in Chemistry

What is a Chain Reaction?

A chain reaction is a fascinating chemical or nuclear process in which one reaction step triggers another, and that one triggers the next, and so on ‚Äî continuing like a row of dominoes falling. You can think of it like lighting one match, and that single flame sets off an entire line of fireworks ‚Äî one small start leads to a massive, self-sustaining series of events.

How does the chain reaction work?

In chemistry, chain reactions often involve radicals ‚Äî highly reactive species with unpaired electrons. The reaction begins with an initiation step, usually sparked by heat, light, or another energy source, which creates the first reactive species. Then comes the propagation phase, where these reactive intermediates generate more of themselves by reacting with other molecules. This keeps the chain going. Eventually, the chain ends in a termination step, where the radicals combine or get neutralized, stopping the process. Chain reactions are not just in chemistry textbooks ‚Äî they power real-life phenomena! Combustion, polymerization, and even explosions are all examples. In nuclear reactions, like those in power plants or atomic bombs, one neutron starts a chain of nuclear fissions, releasing more neutrons and huge amounts of energy. In short, chain reactions are nature‚Äôs way of turning a spark into a spectacle.

Types of Chain Reactions:

  • Chemical Chain Reactions:

  • Involve reactive chemical species such as atoms, molecules, or free radicals.
  • Typically proceed through three key stages: initiation, propagation, and termination

    • Example: Combustion

  • Initiation: A spark or heat source generates free radicals.
  • Propagation: These radicals react with stable molecules to produce more radicals, sustaining the reaction.
  • Termination: Radicals combine to form stable products, ending the chain reaction.

  • Nuclear Chain Reactions:

  • Involve the fission of atomic nuclei, where one nuclear reaction produces neutrons that trigger further fission events.
  • Can be controlled (as in nuclear reactors) or uncontrolled (as in atomic bombs).
  • Release an immense amount of energy.

    • Example: Chernobyl Disaster

  • A neutron collided with Uranium-235, causing it to split and release energy along with additional neutrons.
  • These neutrons caused further fission, creating a self-sustaining chain reaction.
  • In the Chernobyl incident, this reaction became uncontrolled due to flawed reactor design and a failed safety test, resulting in a steam explosion, core meltdown, and widespread radiation release.

  • Polymerization chain reactions.

  • Commonly used in the production of plastics and synthetic resins.
  • Involve the sequential addition of monomers to a growing polymer chain.
  • Typically initiated by a free radical or ion, and proceeds through a chain mechanism

    • Example: Styrene Gas Leak ‚Äì Vizag, 2020 (India)

  • Involved the uncontrolled polymerization of styrene, a monomer used in plastics
  • Though not a classical chain reaction, it serves as a modern example of runaway polymerization due to poor storage conditions

  • Runaway Exothermic Reactions (Not true chain reactions)

  • Characterized by a self-accelerating thermal process, where heat produced by the reaction increases the reaction rate, leading to more heat and pressure.
  • These are often mistaken for chain reactions, but lack the radical or neutronbased self-propagation.

    • Example: Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)

  • Water inadvertently entered a tank containing Methyl Isocyanate (MIC)
  • MIC reacted exothermically with water, generating intense heat and toxic gas
  • The absence of proper cooling systems and pressure relief led to a massive gas leak
  • Though not a chain reaction scientifically, it was a classic example of a runaway chemical reaction, resulting in over 15,000 deaths and long-term health and environmental damage.

    Real-Life Example 2: Chernobyl Disaster

    The Chernobyl disaster occurred on April 26, 1986, at Reactor 4 of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant near Pripyat, Ukraine (then part of the Soviet Union). It stands as one of the most severe nuclear accidents in history.

    Type of Reaction: Nuclear Chain Reaction:
     The reactor operated on the principle of nuclear fission chain reactions. When a neutron strikes a Uranium-235 nucleus, the nucleus splits (fission), releasing energy and additional neutrons. These neutrons go on to split more uranium atoms, maintaining a self-sustaining chain reaction

    What Led to the Cascade?

  • A safety test was conducted under dangerously unstable conditions. Key safety systems were deliberately disabled.
  • Operators removed too many control rods, which are designed to slow or stop the reaction.
  • The reactor had serious design flaws, especially the RBMK reactor‚Äôs positive void coefficient, making it unstable at low power.
  • The chain reaction accelerated uncontrollably, generating immense heat. This led to a massive steam explosion, followed by a core meltdown.
  • The reactor building was destroyed, releasing large amounts of radioactive isotopes (including Iodine-131 and Cesium-137) into the environment.
